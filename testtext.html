I found the reading to be fabulously interesting in regards to its main point that the emergence of mobile technology and innovation sparked a revolution in many aspects, including business, ethics, sociology, and even the human condition. The first article, "Controlled Consumption Culture: When Digital Culture Becomes Software Business, Soren Bro Pold and Christian Ulrik Andersen", describes the interesting nature people have been seen with how consumers use and manipulate the mobile platform. The "digital culture" has disrupted the typical notion of culture, and has fundamentally changed how people have perceived such aspects as intellectual rights, property, media, communication, innovation, and consumer needs. Consequently, there are new business ventures, models, and company/software infrastructures that have formed in the last decade or so. While early on engaged upon as a tool for artistic experimentation, it has blossomed into a full on business with new tools, features, and uses to be sold on the market. The Apple Store has hosted many of these new "apps" and has induced a sort of controlled consumption that is made up of 4 parts: 1. an infrastructure to hold up this e-commerce 2. consumption monitored closely to look for marketing and product patterns 3. limits placed on products to control obsolescense 4. it controls our everyday use and organization of the mobile platforms. It concludes with our need to reflect and discuss this emerging digital culture, its impact on the average consumer, and the many aspects of freedom, privacy and ethics in which we find ourselves struggling with today in other areas.

The other portion of the reading, "Auxiliary Organs, The Ethics of an Extended Mind", revolves around how we as humans interpret our being as the tools of our survival, and how some argue that in this digital era, our adaptation to the modern world has been attributed to the tools of the emerging mobile technology. In this reading, it defines our auxiliary organs as a "platform" and not just a single-function tool. In this, it extendes the "single-function tools" as our everyday "apps".
